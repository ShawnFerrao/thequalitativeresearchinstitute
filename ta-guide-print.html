Coding Reliability TA
"How can we categorise these responses consistently?"
CR
When to use it
  • Multi-site studies where consistency across research teams matters
  • Applied or evaluation research requiring replicable categorisation
  • Research where stakeholders value reliability metrics
  • Large datasets where systematic, rule-based coding is necessary
  • Studies framed within a post-positivist paradigm
What it looks like in practice
  • A codebook is developed (often prior to analysis) with clear definitions and decision rules
  • Two or more coders apply the codebook independently to the same data
  • Inter-rater reliability is calculated (Cohen's kappa, percentage agreement)
  • Discrepancies are resolved through consensus or adjudication

Using inter-rater reliability language while conducting what is actually Reflexive TA. If your coding is evolving and personal, IRR is a quality marker for a different approach.

Codebook / Coding Frame TA
"Can we map these patterns in a way others can follow and verify?"
CF
When to use it
  • Applied, policy, or organisational research where findings must be auditable
  • Multi-researcher teams that need a shared analytic framework
  • Studies where stakeholders require transparency about how categories were derived
  • Research requiring comparability across datasets or time-points
  • Work within a constructivist-pragmatist paradigm
What it looks like in practice
  • A codebook is developed collaboratively and refined as analysis proceeds
  • Team members code independently and compare; differences are discussed, not measured statistically
  • Member checking or peer debriefing may be used to support credibility
  • Reflexive memos track how interpretive decisions were made

Reflexive TA
"What meanings are these people making, and what can I say about why?"
RTA
When to use it
  • Studies exploring how people make meaning of lived experience
  • Solo or small-team research where the researcher's interpretation is central
  • Work within a constructivist or interpretivist paradigm
  • Research where the goal is a rich, contextual, theoretically-engaged account
  • Studies where researcher positionality shapes the analysis
What it looks like in practice
  • Researcher reads data deeply and generates initial codes that reflect their engagement
  • Codes are personal and evolving — not shared for reliability checking
  • Themes are constructed through an iterative, non-linear process
  • A reflexive journal documents interpretive moves and researcher positioning

Claiming Reflexive TA while using inter-rater reliability or a fixed codebook — these are incompatible. Reflexive TA treats researcher subjectivity as a strength; applying reliability checks contradicts its epistemological foundations.

Common pitfalls

Signs your analytic approach
needs clarification

7 / 8

These are not signs your study is flawed — they are signs your analytic logic needs to be made more explicit. Most can be resolved through a clearer articulation of your choices.

Mismatch
Using inter-rater reliability while claiming to do Reflexive TA
IRR is a quality marker for Coding Reliability TA. Reflexive TA treats researcher subjectivity as a resource — applying reliability checks contradicts its epistemological foundations.
Mismatch
Calling themes "patterns of shared meaning" but treating them as topic summaries
Language from Reflexive TA with practices from Coding Reliability TA creates incoherence that reviewers and examiners will notice and question.
Mismatch
Using a fixed codebook while describing coding as reflexive and organic
A fixed codebook is a feature of Coding Reliability or Codebook TA. Reflexive TA coding is flexible and evolving — the two are not compatible in the same study.
Mismatch
Claiming inductive analysis while applying a predefined framework
Clarify whether your framework was developed from the data or applied to it — and name the approach that fits. Inductive vs deductive is separate from which TA approach you use.
Mismatch
Receiving conflicting advice from supervisors or reviewers
This often means different people are assuming different TA approaches. Making your approach explicit — and why it fits your study — usually resolves the confusion quickly.
Mismatch
Feeling unsure how to justify your analytic choices in writing
If you cannot complete the coherence statement on the next page, the issue is conceptual, not empirical — not more data or more codes, but a clearer articulation of your approach.

Putting it together

Can you complete this sentence?

8 / 8

When you can write this sentence clearly and completely, your analytic approach is coherent. If you get stuck on any blank, that points to exactly what needs clarification before you write up your methods.

I am using type of TA to analytic aim because reason it fits my study. This means I understand themes as your definition, coding as your function for coding, and quality as your criteria.
Coding Reliability TA
"I am using Coding Reliability TA to systematically categorise participant responses because consistency across coders is essential for our multi-site study. This means I understand themes as categories, coding as a structured and consistent process, and quality as inter-rater reliability."
Codebook TA
"I am using Codebook TA to map patterned responses across policy contexts because the research needs to be auditable and usable by stakeholders. This means I understand themes as patterned responses, coding as a shared evolving framework, and quality as transparency and traceable decisions."
Reflexive TA
"I am using Reflexive TA to interpret the meaning people make of their experiences because conceptual insight matters more than categorisation. This means I understand themes as patterns of shared meaning I generate, coding as a flexible interpretive process, and quality as reflexivity and depth."
